⚔️ The Greco-Persian Wars — Battle by Battle
499–493 BCE — The Spark

The Ionian Revolt

Greek city-states on the coast of modern Turkey (Ionia) revolted against Persian rule. Athens sent ships to help them. The revolt failed, but it gave Darius I his reason to invade Greece.

490 BCE — First Invasion

Battle of Marathon

Darius I sent a fleet to punish Athens. Despite being heavily outnumbered, Athenian forces used a brilliant flanking tactic — attacking at a run and enveloping the Persian center. Persia retreated. A runner legendarily sprinted ~26 miles to Athens to announce the victory, inspiring the modern marathon race.

480 BCE — Second Invasion

Battle of Thermopylae ("The 300")

Xerxes I led a massive invasion force into Greece. King Leonidas I of Sparta held the narrow mountain pass of Thermopylae with just 300 Spartans and thousands of Greek allies for three days. A traitor revealed a hidden path around the pass, allowing Persia to win — but the sacrifice bought Greece crucial time to prepare. Leonidas died fighting.

480 BCE — Naval Turning Point

Battle of Salamis

Athenian commander Themistocles lured the enormous Persian fleet into the narrow Salamis strait. In the tight waters, the smaller, more maneuverable Greek ships devastated the Persians. This was the only major naval battle of the war — and it crippled Persia's ability to supply its army.

479 BCE — Final Victory

Battle of Plataea

A united Greek army — Spartan-led on land — crushed the remaining Persian forces. The Persians retreated and never again seriously threatened mainland Greece. Greece had won the war.

🏆 Who Won & What Happened After

Greece Won Against All Odds

Outnumbered in nearly every engagement, the united Greek city-states turned back the world's largest empire through superior tactics, terrain knowledge, and naval strategy. The victories at Marathon, Salamis, and Plataea are still studied in military academies today.

The Golden Age of Athens

Greek victory sparked an explosion of confidence. Under the leader Pericles, Athens entered its Golden Age (~480–404 BCE) — an era of democracy, philosophy (Socrates, Plato, Aristotle), stunning architecture (the Parthenon), and art that shaped all of Western civilization.

The End of the Persian Empire

The Persian Empire lasted until 330 BCE, when Alexander the Great of Macedonia swept through it in just a few years — defeating Darius III in battle and burning Persepolis to the ground. Over 200 years of Achaemenid Persian rule ended.

🗺️ The Persian Empire — Overview

What Was the Persian Empire?

The Persian Empire (also called the Achaemenid Empire) was the largest empire the ancient world had ever seen. Founded around 550 BCE by Cyrus the Great, it stretched from modern-day Iran across the Middle East, Egypt, Central Asia, and into northwest India — covering about 5.5 million square kilometers at its peak.

📍 Where Was It Located?

The empire centered on modern-day Iran but spread across a huge swath of the ancient world:

  • West: Modern Turkey, Egypt, and parts of Libya
  • East: Modern Afghanistan and Pakistan (northwest India)
  • North: Modern Armenia, Azerbaijan, and parts of Central Asia
  • South: The Persian Gulf coast of modern Saudi Arabia
👑 The Major Emperors

Cyrus the Great (~550 BCE)

Founded the Persian Empire by unifying the Medes and Persians. Famous for his remarkable tolerance of conquered peoples — he let them keep their religions, customs, and local leaders. He freed the Jews from Babylonian captivity, earning him praise even in the Bible.

Darius I — "Darius the Great" (~522–486 BCE)

The great organizer and builder. Created the Royal Road (1,500-mile highway), built Persepolis, divided the empire into provinces (satrapies), and established Zoroastrianism as the state religion. Led the first invasion of Greece (490 BCE).

Xerxes I (~486–465 BCE)

Son of Darius. Led the massive second invasion of Greece (480 BCE) — the largest military campaign the ancient world had seen. Famous for facing the 300 Spartans at Thermopylae and later losing at Salamis and Plataea.

🕊️ What Made Persia Unique

The First Multicultural Empire

No empire before Persia had governed such diverse peoples — different languages, religions, and cultures — under one central government while allowing local customs to continue. Conquered peoples could keep their religions, languages, and local governors (called satraps). This tolerance was revolutionary.

Religion: Zoroastrianism

The Persian Empire's primary religion was Zoroastrianism, founded by the prophet Zoroaster. It focused on the cosmic struggle between good (Ahura Mazda) and evil (Angra Mainyu) — one of the world's first monotheistic-leaning religions. Darius I made it the official state religion. Policies on non-followers varied by ruler — some enforced it strictly, while Cyrus was notably tolerant.

Famous Public Works

  • The Royal Road: A 1,500-mile highway from Sardis (Turkey) to Susa (Iran). Royal couriers could travel it in 7 days — a journey that normally took 90 days.
  • Persepolis: A spectacular ceremonial capital city with massive palaces, audience halls, and carvings celebrating the empire's power.

Well Known For...

  • Tolerance of conquered peoples — unprecedented in history
  • Massive infrastructure (Royal Road, Persepolis)
  • A powerful professional army with archers and cavalry
  • An efficient provincial government system (satraps)
  • A vast postal/messenger relay system on the Royal Road
